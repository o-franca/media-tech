# How to Build an App for Your Small Business: A Practical Guide for Ireland & UK

You have an idea for an app. Maybe it is a booking system for your salon in Cork, a loyalty programme for your cafe in London, or a marketplace connecting local service providers in Dublin. The question is: where do you start, and how much is it going to cost?

Building an app used to mean hiring a development team, spending tens of thousands of euros, and waiting months for a finished product. That has changed dramatically. Today, small businesses in Ireland and the UK have more options than ever, from no-code platforms that let you build an app in a weekend to lean MVP approaches that validate your idea before you invest heavily.

This guide walks you through the entire process, from deciding whether you actually need an app to launching it in the App Store and Google Play.

## Do You Actually Need an App?

Before you spend a single euro or pound, ask yourself an honest question: does your business genuinely need a mobile app, or would a well-built mobile website serve your customers just as well?

An app makes sense when:

- **Your customers need to interact with you frequently.** If people use your service daily or weekly, an app provides a smoother, faster experience than a website.
- **You need offline functionality.** If your product needs to work without an internet connection, an app is the right choice.
- **Push notifications are valuable.** If timely alerts (order updates, appointment reminders, special offers) are central to your customer experience, an app delivers them more effectively.
- **You are building a community or marketplace.** Apps with social features, user profiles, and real-time interactions tend to perform better as native applications.

A mobile website makes more sense when:

- Your customers interact with you occasionally or for one-off transactions.
- Your primary goal is providing information (menus, opening hours, pricing).
- Your budget is very limited and you need to start generating results immediately.

If you are still not sure, build a mobile-optimised website first. You can always develop an app later once you have validated demand.

## The Three Paths to Building an App

There are three main approaches to building an app for your small business. Each has trade-offs in cost, speed, quality, and flexibility.

### Path 1: No-Code and Low-Code Platforms

No-code platforms let you build functional apps using visual interfaces, drag-and-drop components, and pre-built templates. You do not need to write a single line of code.

**Best for:** Simple apps with standard functionality like bookings, directories, e-commerce, content delivery, and basic social features.

**Popular platforms for Irish and UK small businesses:**

- **Glide** turns spreadsheet data into polished mobile apps. Excellent for internal tools, directories, and simple customer-facing apps.
- **Adalo** offers more design flexibility and supports both iOS and Android with native-feeling apps.
- **FlutterFlow** provides a visual builder on top of Flutter, giving you more power while still avoiding raw code.
- **Bubble** is technically a web app builder but can be wrapped for mobile. Best for complex logic and data-heavy applications.

**Cost range:** Free tiers available for testing. Paid plans typically run from 25 to 150 euros per month depending on features and user volume.

**Timeline:** A simple app can be built in one to four weeks.

**Limitations:** No-code apps can feel slower than native apps, customisation is limited to what the platform supports, and you are dependent on the platform continuing to operate and support your chosen features.

### Path 2: Hybrid Development

Hybrid frameworks like React Native and Flutter let developers write code once and deploy to both iOS and Android. This is a middle ground between no-code simplicity and fully native performance.

**Best for:** Apps that need custom functionality, smooth performance, and a polished user experience, but where building separate iOS and Android apps would be too expensive.

**Cost range:** Hiring a freelance developer in Ireland or the UK for a hybrid app typically costs between 5,000 and 25,000 euros depending on complexity. Agencies tend to charge more, often starting at 15,000 euros for a basic app.

**Timeline:** Two to four months for an MVP.

### Path 3: Native Development

Native apps are built specifically for iOS (using Swift) or Android (using Kotlin). They offer the best performance and the deepest integration with device features.

**Best for:** Apps where performance is critical, you need advanced device features (camera, GPS, sensors), or you are building a product where the app itself is the business.

**Cost range:** Native development typically starts at 15,000 euros per platform and can easily reach 50,000 euros or more for complex applications.

**Timeline:** Three to six months per platform for an MVP.

## The MVP Approach: Build Less, Learn More

The biggest mistake I see small businesses make when building apps is trying to include every feature from day one. This approach is slow, expensive, and risky because you are investing heavily before you know whether customers actually want what you are building.

The MVP (Minimum Viable Product) approach flips this. You build the smallest possible version of your app that delivers value, launch it, gather feedback, and iterate based on real user behaviour.

### How to define your MVP

1. **List every feature you want in your dream app.** Write them all down without filtering.
2. **Categorise each feature.** Mark each one as "must have for launch," "nice to have," or "future phase."
3. **Be ruthless with the must-have list.** Your MVP should have three to five core features, not fifteen. Ask yourself: if the app only did this one thing, would people still use it?
4. **Define your success metric.** What does success look like for your MVP? Is it 100 downloads? 50 active weekly users? 20 bookings? Set a clear target before you build.

### Lessons from building Dine With Me

When I built Dine With Me, a social dining app for iOS and Android, the temptation was to launch with every feature we had imagined: group bookings, restaurant reviews, social profiles, messaging, payment splitting, and more.

Instead, we launched with a focused MVP that solved one core problem exceptionally well. That discipline forced us to understand what users actually valued before investing in additional features. Many of the features we thought were essential turned out to be unnecessary, and some features users loved were ones we had not even considered initially.

This experience shapes how I advise every small business that wants to build an app. Start lean. Let your users tell you what to build next.

## Finding and Working with Developers in Ireland and the UK

If you decide to hire a developer or agency, here is how to approach the process.

### Where to find developers

- **Freelance platforms.** Upwork, Toptal, and PeoplePerHour are popular in Ireland and the UK. Look for developers with verified reviews and portfolio pieces relevant to your industry.
- **Local tech communities.** Cork, Dublin, Belfast, London, Manchester, and Edinburgh all have active tech meetup scenes. Attending events is a great way to meet developers and get referrals.
- **University partnerships.** Several Irish and UK universities run programmes where computer science students work on real projects. This can be cost-effective for simpler apps.
- **Agency directories.** Clutch.co and GoodFirms list agencies by location, specialisation, and client reviews.

### What to look for in a developer

- **Relevant portfolio.** Have they built apps similar to what you need? Ask to see live examples you can download and test.
- **Communication.** The developer who communicates clearly and asks good questions about your business is almost always a better choice than the cheapest option.
- **Post-launch support.** Apps need ongoing maintenance, bug fixes, and updates. Make sure your agreement covers what happens after launch.
- **Transparent pricing.** Be wary of developers who give you a fixed price without asking detailed questions about what you want. Good developers will scope the project carefully before quoting.

### Managing the development process

- **Use a written brief.** Document what your app should do, who it is for, and what success looks like. The more detailed your brief, the fewer surprises during development.
- **Agree on milestones.** Break the project into phases with specific deliverables. Pay based on milestones rather than all upfront.
- **Test early and often.** Do not wait until the app is "finished" to start testing. Review builds every one to two weeks and provide feedback.
- **Involve real users.** Get potential customers to test early versions. Their feedback is more valuable than yours because they see the app with fresh eyes.

## Understanding Costs for the Irish and UK Market

App development costs vary significantly based on complexity, platform, and who builds it. Here is a realistic breakdown for 2026.

### No-code app
- **Build cost:** 0 to 2,000 euros (your time plus platform subscription)
- **Monthly running cost:** 25 to 150 euros
- **Best for:** Testing an idea, internal tools, simple customer apps

### Simple hybrid app (freelancer)
- **Build cost:** 5,000 to 15,000 euros
- **Monthly running cost:** 100 to 500 euros (hosting, maintenance, updates)
- **Best for:** Customer-facing apps with moderate complexity

### Complex hybrid app (agency)
- **Build cost:** 15,000 to 50,000 euros
- **Monthly running cost:** 500 to 2,000 euros
- **Best for:** Apps with custom backends, integrations, and advanced features

### Native app (per platform)
- **Build cost:** 15,000 to 50,000+ euros per platform
- **Monthly running cost:** 500 to 3,000 euros
- **Best for:** Performance-critical apps, complex user experiences

Remember that Apple charges 99 euros per year for an App Store developer account, and Google charges a one-time fee of 25 dollars for Google Play.

## Launching and Growing Your App

Building the app is only half the battle. Getting people to download and use it is the other half.

### Pre-launch checklist

- **Create a landing page.** Build a simple page that explains what your app does and collects email addresses from interested people. See how I approach [landing page design for product launches](/cases/landing-page.html).
- **Set up analytics.** Install tracking from day one so you can measure downloads, active users, retention, and key actions within your app.
- **Prepare your App Store listing.** Write a compelling description, create screenshots that show the app in action, and choose the right category and keywords.
- **Plan your launch promotion.** Email your list, post on social media, reach out to local press and bloggers, and consider running targeted ads in your area.

### Post-launch growth strategies

- **Ask for reviews.** App Store ratings heavily influence download rates. Prompt happy users to leave a review at the right moment, typically after a positive interaction.
- **Iterate based on data.** Watch how people actually use your app. Where do they drop off? What features do they use most? Let the data guide your roadmap.
- **Build referral mechanics.** Give users a reason to invite friends. Discounts, exclusive content, or enhanced features for referrals can drive organic growth.
- **Keep updating.** Apps that stop getting updates feel abandoned. Regular improvements, even small ones, signal to users and the app stores that your product is actively maintained.

## Start With the Problem, Not the Technology

The most important piece of advice I can give any small business owner considering an app is this: start with the problem you are solving, not the technology you want to use.

Talk to your customers. Understand their frustrations. Identify the specific moment where an app would make their experience meaningfully better. Build for that moment first, and expand from there.

Whether you use a no-code builder over a weekend or invest in a professionally developed native app, the businesses that succeed are the ones that stay focused on delivering genuine value to their users.
